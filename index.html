What is the Jevons Paradox?

The Jevons Paradox (or Rebound Effect) describes a counterintuitive economic phenomenon: when a resource becomes cheaper to use through efficiency improvements, total consumption of that resource increases rather than decreasing. The efficiency gain lowers barriers, expands the market, and demand explodes past the efficiency savings.

This investigation tests whether this paradox applies to AI compute. As GPU/TPU architectures have improved dramatically — through Tensor cores, FP16 precision, and optimized memory bandwidth — has the AI industry's total energy footprint shrunk, or grown?

We synthesize four datasets covering hardware efficiency, frontier model training, data center infrastructure, and 3,237 model deployments to answer this question through three testable hypotheses.

Methodological Note — Metric Shift
Pre-2017 hardware efficiency is measured in FP32/TDP (single-precision floating-point). Post-2017 chips use Tensor-FP16/TDP, reflecting specialized deep learning hardware. These are not identical operations. Regression analysis is confined to the post-2017 cohort for consistency, and the two eras are annotated separately in all visualizations. Future projections (post-2026) are excluded entirely.

Before claiming a paradox, we must first confirm the efficiency premise. The ml_hardware dataset provides the answer.

The regression result

Log-linear OLS regression of FLOP/W against year (2017–2025, n=61) reveals a highly significant positive trend. The year variable alone explains 54.9% of variance in log-efficiency — a strong result given the diversity of chip manufacturers and architectures in the dataset.

The demand regression

Log-linear regression of training compute against publication year produces an almost perfect fit: R²=0.893 with p<10⁻¹⁹. Compute is doubling every 11.5 months. Three forces drive this: more GPUs per cluster, longer training runs, and development budgets growing 2–3× annually.

Mann-Whitney U test

A t-test would be inappropriate for training compute data — values span many orders of magnitude and are strongly non-normal. The Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric, rank-based) comparing pre-2020 vs. post-2020 models yields:

The data center infrastructure confirms this: 23 frontier clusters grew from near-zero in early 2023 to 751 MW by October 2025.

Model-level anchors

BERT's training run in 2019 (Strubell et al.) consumed roughly 1,507 kWh and generated ~0.65 tCO₂e — the carbon footprint of a single long-haul flight. GPT-3's 2020 training run consumed 1,287 MWh and generated ~552 tCO₂e. This 847× emissions jump occurred in a single model generation.

It is important to note that architectural improvements, efficient hardware selection, and optimal data center location can reduce a specific training run by 100–1,000×. Yet the aggregate data shows this is insufficient — the scale of training is growing faster than any per-run optimization can offset.

The all_ai_models dataset (3,237 entries) shows the maximum power draw per individual training run grew from ~38,000 W in 2016 to ~110,000,000 W in 2025. This 2,895× increase in peak power demand over nine years is the direct physical manifestation of H2's demand explosion translating into real-world infrastructure.

§ 5 · Conclusion

The Inescapable Math
of AI Scaling

"Efficiency improves at a doubling rate of 3.6 years — but compute demand doubles every 0.96 years. Demand is actively outpacing efficiency growth by a factor of 3.75×."

This investigation empirically confirms all three hypotheses, establishing the Jevons Paradox within the modern AI sector. The chain is complete and quantified across all four datasets.

Component-level efficiency improvements are real and valuable, but cannot close a 3.75× structural gap between the supply of efficiency and the demand for computation. Unless aggregate scaling is subject to structural constraints — hard compute caps, regulatory limits on cluster size, or a fundamental paradigm shift — the environmental footprint of AI will continue to compound regardless of hardware progress.

The math is inescapable.
